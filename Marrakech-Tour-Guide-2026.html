The Red City

Marrakech at a Glance

Marrakech is Morocco's most visited city — a place where ancient Berber culture collides with French colonial elegance and contemporary luxury. Founded in 1070 by the Almoravid dynasty, the city has served for nearly a thousand years as a crossroads of trade, art, spirituality, and power.

Today it draws over 15 million visitors a year: honeymooners enchanted by rose-scented riads, digital nomads working from rooftop cafés, luxury travelers at La Mamounia, and backpackers losing themselves in the souks. No city in Africa rewards the curious traveler more generously.

The medina — the ancient walled city — is a UNESCO World Heritage Site. Beyond its walls, modern Gueliz and chic Hivernage offer a very different Marrakech: wine bars, contemporary art galleries, and rooftop pools.

Marrakech Neighbourhoods

The city is a patchwork of distinct districts — each with its own character, pace, and hidden rewards.

01 · Medina

The Medina

The ancient walled heart of Marrakech. A UNESCO World Heritage labyrinth of souks, mosques, riads, and fountains. The most immersive place to stay in the city.

Best for: Authentic experience
02 · Gueliz

Gueliz

The French-built new city. Wide boulevards, contemporary restaurants, art galleries, boutiques, and the best café culture outside Paris. Perfect for design lovers.

Best for: Modern luxury & nightlife
03 · Kasbah

Kasbah & Mellah

The southern medina quarter, home to the Saadian Tombs, Bahia Palace, and the historic Jewish quarter (Mellah). Quieter and more residential than the northern medina.

Best for: History & culture
04 · Mouassine

Mouassine Quarter

The trendy creative heart of the medina. Concept stores, art galleries, Le Jardin Secret, and the city's most photogenic fountains and passages. Great restaurant scene.

Best for: Boutiques & dining
05 · Hivernage

Hivernage

The luxury hotel district adjacent to Gueliz. Home to La Mamounia, Es Saadi, and the city's upscale spas, garden estates, and five-star rooftop pools.

Best for: Luxury & wellness
06 · Palmeraie

La Palmeraie

The palm grove district north of the medina. Desert-edge luxury villas, quad biking, camel rides, golf courses, and some of Morocco's most spectacular boutique hotels.

Best for: Desert vibe & adventure
Must-See Attractions

Top Landmarks & Sights

From UNESCO monuments to secret gardens — every essential sight in Marrakech, with insider tips on when and how to visit.

01 · Must-See

Jemaa el-Fnaa Square

The pulsing heart of Marrakech — UNESCO Intangible Cultural Heritage and one of the world's great public squares. By day: orange juice vendors, henna artists, and snake charmers. By night: open-air kitchens, Gnawa musicians, fire-eaters, and storytellers performing in Darija to rapt crowds.

Insider Tip: Arrive at 6pm to watch the daily transformation begin. Climb to any rooftop café on the square's perimeter for a bird's-eye view with mint tea — Café de France or Café Glacier are classics.

"The square doesn't just show you Marrakech — it is Marrakech. Every sense overwhelmed, every moment unforgettable."

Free entryOpen 24hrsBest at dusk
02 · Iconic Garden

Jardin Majorelle

Created by French painter Jacques Majorelle in the 1920s, restored by Yves Saint Laurent and Pierre Bergé, and now one of Morocco's most visited sites. The electric cobalt "Majorelle Blue" villa against 300 exotic plant species is jaw-dropping. The adjacent Berber Museum (Musée Yves Saint Laurent) is world-class.

Insider Tip: Book online — it sells out daily. Visit at 8am opening time for empty alleys and perfect photographs. Budget 2 hours minimum including the museum.

"A surrealist painting brought to life — part botanical garden, part art installation."

Book ahead~150 MADOpen daily 8am–6pm
03 · Royal Palace

Bahia Palace

Built in the late 19th century for Grand Vizier Si Moussa, Bahia Palace is 8 hectares of pure opulence — 150 rooms, hand-painted cedarwood ceilings, zellij marble floors, and orange-blossom courtyard gardens. "Bahia" means brilliance, and it doesn't disappoint. Don't miss the intricate plasterwork in the grand riad courtyard.

Insider Tip: Hire a licensed guide at the entrance — the palace's stories of power, jealousy, and court intrigue are as compelling as its architecture. Visit on weekday mornings to avoid tour groups.

"Every corridor is a new wonder — Moroccan craftsmanship at its absolute zenith."

~70 MADMon–Sat 9am–5pmGuide recommended
What to Do

Unforgettable Experiences

Beyond the sights — the activities, rituals, and encounters that make Marrakech truly transformative.

Traditional Hammam

A hammam — steam, black beldi soap, and kessa scrub — is a ritual Marrakchis have practiced for centuries. From humble neighbourhood bathhouses to five-star spa experiences at La Mamounia, this is the most Moroccan thing you can do.

  • Neighbourhood hammam: 30–80 MAD
  • Luxury spa hammam: 400–1,200 MAD
  • Hammam Dar el-Bacha (authentic, locals)
  • Book spa hammams 48hrs ahead

Riad Stay

Staying in a riad — a traditional inward-facing guesthouse built around a courtyard — is the defining Marrakech experience. Wake to bird song, breakfasts served under orange trees, and rooftop sunsets with mint tea. Medina magic that no hotel can replicate.

  • Budget riads from 400 MAD/night
  • Luxury riads: 2,000–8,000 MAD/night
  • Book at least 2 months ahead (peak season)
  • Mouassine & Bab Doukkala best areas

Souk Deep-Dive

The Marrakech souks are organized by trade in the medina's northern quarter — spices, leather, lanterns, carpets, textiles, silver. Losing yourself deliberately for an afternoon is the city's greatest gift. Have a riad address written in Arabic to find your way back.

  • Souk Semmarine: textiles & carpets
  • Rahba Kedima: spices & apothecary
  • Souk Cherratine: leather goods
  • Bargain: start at 40% of asking price

Agafay Desert Night

Only 30 minutes from the city, the rocky Agafay Desert offers Saharan grandeur without the 10-hour drive. Book a luxury glamping camp for the night — camel rides at sunset, tagine under the stars, Berber music around a fire, and absolute silence.

  • Day trip: camel ride + sunset dinner
  • Overnight glamping: 1,200–3,500 MAD
  • Scarabeo Camp & Agafay Desert Camp
  • Best: October–April

Moroccan Cooking Class

Learn to make tagine, couscous, and pastilla from a Marrakchi home cook or in a medina kitchen school. Most classes include a souk tour to buy ingredients, then 3–4 hours cooking and eating. One of the best ways to understand Moroccan culture through its food.

  • La Maison Arabe: classic cooking school
  • Souk Cuisine: market + cook experience
  • 250–650 MAD per person
  • Book 3+ days ahead for good classes

Rooftop Sundowner

Watching the sun set over the medina from a rooftop — mint tea or a cold drink in hand, as the Koutoubia minaret turns gold and the muezzin calls echo — is one of travel's great moments. Nomad, Café Arabe, and Cheryl are the finest vantage points.

  • Nomad: modern Moroccan cuisine + views
  • Café Arabe: Italian-Moroccan fusion
  • Le Jardin: beautiful garden setting
  • Always book ahead in peak season
What to Eat

The Food Guide to Marrakech

Moroccan cuisine is one of the world's great culinary traditions. Here is everything you must eat, drink, and experience in Marrakech.

Main Dish

Tagine

Morocco's slow-cooked stew — chicken with preserved lemon and olives, lamb with prunes and almonds, or kefta with egg — served in the iconic conical clay pot that has defined Moroccan cooking for centuries.

Best at: Chez Driss, Jemaa el-Fnaa stalls, Le Tobsil
Celebratory Dish

Pastilla

The masterpiece of Moroccan cuisine — layers of warqa (gossamer pastry) filled with spiced pigeon or chicken, almonds, and egg, dusted with powdered sugar and cinnamon. The sweet-savoury contrast is unforgettable.

Best at: Dar Moha, La Maison Arabe, Dar Yacout
Friday Ritual

Couscous

Traditionally served on Fridays after the midday prayer. Semolina steamed over a slow-cooked broth of vegetables and meat (lamb, chicken, or merguez), topped with caramelized onions and raisins. A communal family dish of great importance.

Best on: Fridays, family restaurants in Gueliz
Soup

Harira

A rich, tomato-lentil-chickpea soup scented with coriander, saffron, and cinnamon. The Ramadan soup par excellence — but served year-round. Particularly nourishing on cool winter evenings. Usually eaten with dates and chebbakia (honey pastry).

Best at: Jemaa el-Fnaa stalls after dark, Café de France
Street Food

Msemen & Baghrir

Msemen (flaky, buttery flatbread) and baghrir (thousand-hole semolina pancakes drizzled with honey and argan oil) are the definitive Marrakchi breakfast — eaten at tiny plastic tables with a glass of sweet mint tea.

Best at: neighbourhood bakeries, Café Clock
Sacred Ritual

Mint Tea (Atay)

Morocco's national drink and primary social ritual. Fresh spearmint, gunpowder green tea, and mountains of sugar, poured from a great height into small glasses to create a froth. Accept every offer — refusing tea is refusing hospitality.

Everywhere, always, all day
Condiment / Dip

Argan Oil & Amlou

Liquid gold from the argan trees of the Sous Valley — nutty, rich, and extraordinary. Amlou is a Berber almond-argan paste mixed with honey, served for breakfast with bread. Buy the real thing from women's cooperatives in the souks.

Buy at: Ensemble Artisanal, souk cooperatives
Sweet

Chebakia & Sellou

Chebakia are sesame-honey pastry roses, fried and coated in orange-blossom syrup — especially beloved during Ramadan. Sellou is a dense, nutritious mixture of toasted flour, almonds, anise, and honey. Both are extraordinary and addictive.

Best at: medina pastry shops, souk stalls

From street-food institutions to rooftop dining rooms — the definitive Marrakech restaurant list for every budget and occasion.

01

Le Tobsil

The most romantic dinner in Marrakech — a beautifully restored riad in the medina where a five-course set menu of traditional Moroccan dishes is served by candlelight. No menu choices, no disappointments. Reservation essential weeks in advance.

Beyond the City

Best Day Trips from Marrakech

Marrakech is the perfect base for exploring southern Morocco. These are the finest day and overnight excursions within reach of the Red City.

30 min · Rock Desert

Agafay Desert

Rocky desert glamour just 30km from Marrakech. Camel rides, quad biking, luxury camps, and the most spectacular sunset in the region. The full Sahara experience — without the 10-hour drive.

⏱ Half day or overnight
45 min · Mountain Range

Atlas Mountains

Toubkal (4,167m — Africa's highest peak) and the Ourika Valley are 45 minutes from the city. Hike Berber villages, visit waterfalls, eat trout by a mountain river, and return to the medina by dinner. Extraordinary contrast.

⏱ Full day or 2-day trek
2.5 hrs · Atlantic Coast

Essaouira

Morocco's most atmospheric coastal city — a white-and-blue UNESCO medina battered by Atlantic winds, legendary for fresh seafood, surf culture, Gnawa music, and an art scene that has drawn artists since the 1960s. A perfect full-day contrast to the inland heat.

⏱ Full day or overnight
3 hrs · Over the Atlas

Ouarzazate & Ait Benhaddou

Cross the High Atlas via the spectacular Tizi n'Tichka pass (2,260m) to reach the "Hollywood of Africa" — Ouarzazate's film studios (Game of Thrones, Gladiator, Lawrence of Arabia) and the breathtaking UNESCO kasbah of Aït Benhaddou.

⏱ Full day or 2-day
35 min · Valley & Waterfall

Ourika Valley

The closest mountain escape — a lush Berber valley following the Ourika River into the High Atlas, dotted with small restaurants by the stream, a saffron cooperative at Tnine, and the Setti Fatma waterfalls at the valley's head (40-minute hike).

⏱ Half day or full day
2.5 hrs · Waterfalls

Ouzoud Waterfalls

Morocco's most spectacular waterfalls — a 110-metre cascade surrounded by olive and carob trees, with Barbary macaques in the canopy, swimming holes below, and basic restaurants on the cliffside. One of Morocco's most beautiful natural sites.

⏱ Full day

Best Time to Visit Marrakech

Marrakech has a semi-arid climate — brilliant sunshine most of the year, with distinct hot, mild, and cool seasons. Here is what to expect in each.

Spring
March · April · May
22–28°C
Average daytime

The finest season — warm, clear skies, gardens in bloom, long evenings. Crowds build in April; May is particularly beautiful and manageable.

Best Season
Summer
June · July · August
38–44°C
Average daytime

Intensely hot — sightseeing before 11am and after 6pm only. Fewer tourists, lower prices, and spectacular nights when the heat finally relents.

Very Hot
Autumn
Sep · Oct · Nov
24–30°C
Average daytime

A second golden season — October is especially perfect. Warm but not scorching, excellent light for photography, and the desert at its most serene.

Best Season
Winter
Dec · Jan · Feb
16–22°C
Average daytime

Mild days but cold nights (5–8°C) — pack layers. Fewest tourists, best riad prices, and the Atlas Mountains are snow-capped. Atmospheric and underrated.

Low Season
Off the Beaten Track

Marrakech Hidden Gems

The places most tourists never find — and the ones that Marrakech regulars return for every time.

01

Mellah — The Jewish Quarter

Established in 1558, the Mellah was home to one of Morocco's oldest and most vibrant Jewish communities. Today its synagogues, covered market streets, and ornate balconied houses are a poignant and largely tourist-free window into a vanished world.

"The most honest, unpolished corner of the medina."

02

Dar Si Said Museum

A stunning 19th-century palace overlooking a lush garden, housing one of Morocco's finest collections of Moroccan woodwork, weapons, textiles, and ceramics. Almost always empty — a serene counterpoint to the Bahia Palace crowds next door.

"World-class collection, zero queues."

03

Cyber Park Arsat Moulay Abdeslam

A 19th-century royal garden recently restored as a free public park — orange and olive trees, fountains, shaded walkways, and free Wi-Fi. Where Marrakchis actually come to relax on weekends, completely unknown to tourists.

"Where the real Marrakech rests."

04

Fondouk el-Amiri

A 16th-century caravanserai (merchant inn) near the souks, restored as an arts and crafts complex. Woodworkers, weavers, and leather craftsmen work openly in the galleries — a living museum of Moroccan artisanship with no tourist pressure.

"Watch a zellige tile being hand-cut — a lifetime memory."

05

Bab Doukkala Mosque Fountain

A beautifully ornate 16th-century public water fountain — an architectural masterpiece of carved cedar, zellij tiles, and green ceramic roof tiles — hidden on a quiet residential street near the Bab Doukkala gate. Locals walk past it without a second glance.

"The finest public fountain in the medina — completely undiscovered."

06

Café Clock Marrakech

A converted riad in the Kasbah neighbourhood running storytelling nights, Darija language classes, Gnawa music sessions, and a menu featuring Moroccan dishes alongside the best camel burger in the world. A cultural hub and genuine community space.

"The best-kept secret for connecting with the city's creative culture."

Respect & Understanding

Culture & Etiquette Guide

Marrakech rewards travelers who arrive with cultural respect and genuine curiosity. These guidelines will help you navigate the city with confidence and kindness.

Do's — Earn Respect & Connection

🤝
Greet with "As-salamu alaykum" — even a basic Arabic greeting opens doors and warms hearts across the medina. Locals are visibly pleased when visitors make the effort.
🫖
Accept mint tea offers — whether in a shop, a home, or a riad. Accepting tea is accepting hospitality. The tea itself is usually exceptional. Enjoy the ritual.
👟
Remove shoes at doorways — before entering mosques, private homes, and many riads. Look for shoes by the door as your cue. This is one of the most important courtesies.
🎨
Bargain with humour and respect — it is a social ritual, not a battle. Smile, enjoy the exchange, and agree on a price that feels fair to both parties. Never bargain aggressively on something you don't intend to buy.
📸
Always ask before photographing people — particularly at Jemaa el-Fnaa. A smile and a pointed finger at your camera usually gets a nod (and a small tip is appreciated).

Don'ts — Avoid Misunderstandings

👗
Don't wear revealing clothing in the medina — shorts and sleeveless tops are fine in Gueliz and hotel pools, but in the medina both men and women should cover knees and shoulders. This is not optional — it's basic respect.
🚶
Don't follow "unofficial guides" — men who approach you near the medina gates offering free directions almost always lead to a carpet shop. A firm "La shukran" (No thank you) is sufficient and polite.
💰
Don't photograph people for free — particularly snake charmers, water sellers in traditional dress, and acrobats at Jemaa el-Fnaa. These are professional performers who rely on tips. A photograph is worth 5–20 MAD.
🍷
Don't drink alcohol in public — alcohol is available in licensed restaurants, bars, and hotels, but drinking on the street or in public spaces is deeply inappropriate in this predominantly Muslim city.
🕌
Don't try to enter mosques — with the exception of the Koutoubia Mosque gardens, most Moroccan mosques are closed to non-Muslims. Peering in from doorways is considered disrespectful.
Before You Go

Practical Travel Tips

Everything you need to know before arriving — from visas to currency to getting around the medina without getting lost.

Getting There

  • Marrakech Menara Airport (RAK) — 6km from the medina
  • Direct flights from Europe (2–3hrs), no transit needed
  • Grand Taxi from airport: 70–100 MAD (agree fare first)
  • Bus 19 from airport: 30 MAD to Jemaa el-Fnaa
  • No visa needed for EU, USA, UK, Canada visitors

Money & Currency

  • Currency: Moroccan Dirham (MAD) — 1 EUR ≈ 11 MAD
  • Cash is essential in the medina — most souks don't take cards
  • Withdraw from bank ATMs (Attijariwafa, BMCE) — best rates
  • Do not change money on the street — always use banks or ATMs
  • Credit cards accepted in hotels, restaurants in Gueliz

Getting Around

  • Medina: on foot only — alleys too narrow for vehicles
  • Petit Taxi (beige): short trips — always agree fare or use meter
  • Careem / inDriver: ride-hailing apps with fixed prices
  • Calèche (horse-drawn carriage): scenic rides around medina walls
  • Grand Taxi: shared long-distance taxis for day trips

Communication

  • Languages: Darija (Moroccan Arabic), French, Tamazight, English
  • French is widely understood in hotels and restaurants
  • SIM card: Maroc Telecom or inwi — buy at airport (30–60 MAD)
  • Wi-Fi: free at all riads and most cafés
  • Download Google Maps offline before arriving

Safety Tips

  • Marrakech is generally safe — standard urban awareness applies
  • Secure valuables in your riad safe; use a crossbody bag in souks
  • Drink bottled water only — tap water not recommended
  • Agree taxi fares before departure or insist on meter
  • Solo female travelers: confident body language is best deterrent

Health & Essentials

  • No vaccinations required — hepatitis A recommended
  • Travel insurance strongly advised (EHIC not valid in Morocco)
  • Pharmacies everywhere — recognizable by green crescent sign
  • Sunscreen: essential April–October (UV index very high)
  • Emergency: Police 19 · Ambulance 15 · Tourist Police 0524 38 46 01
